I Call a “Do-Over”

Ever play a game of kickball when the person kicking wafted and missed the ball completely, thereby calling, “do-over”? Well sometimes the relationship between a graphic designer and a client is like a game of kickball – Do-overs can save face and produce great results!

As a seasoned graphic designer, I’ve come to believe that the best designs are produced when the graphic designer and the client work together. As the client, be prepared to give feedback as to what you like and what fits your company's image. As the owner of this design you have a personal stake in its success. This makes it sometimes difficult to let go and rely on a designer to take control of your visual image. But good collaboration and communication, as with kickball, are the answers!

For the Client:

Your goal is to make sure you are understood and constructive. The designer should include at least 3 rounds of changes in their design proposal and that should give you plenty of opportunities and options to get what you need. Remember, you don’t have to make any concessions you don’t want.

Begin By Interviewing the Designer:

  • Review the designer’s portfolio and if this is the ‘first’ project, ask him to describe his responsibility for each job and how many concepts were needed to build the final design.
  • Ask how he/she stepped up to the challenge of design critiques.

Initiate the Design:

  • Give examples of other designs you like.
  • Describe the audience and how you want them to feel when they see the design.
  • Set up a few specific rules needed to match brand standards.
  • Give the designer the freedom to communicate and create using his unique ideas.
  • Make sure you have a complete understanding of the final production and deliverable for this design.
  • If the designer plans to give you several concepts, make sure you review each and comment on what you like and dislike.

Providing Feedback: Be very specific about what you do like. But, you DON'T have to be specific about what you don't like.

General statements are enough to let the graphic designer know how to proceed with the next proof. The best way to keep communication efficient is:

  • Keep your written critiques concise.
  • Document all verbal discussions & keep notes.
  • Provide direct and clear instructions.

Let the graphic designer step up and do his job – which is to make you happy.

For Designers Only:

I tell my clients, the worst thing they can do is say nothing. Even if the client says they hate the design, at least I have an example of what not to do. If a client gives me feedback that is not specific I usually jump in with a few questions. What specifically don't you like, is it the color, the font, the photograph or the design style? Usually if you can keep asking questions you can get it narrowed down and know how to do it right the next time! Good designers keep their emotions in check and know that nothing should be taken personally – so, go-ahead and make it a, “Do-over”.
