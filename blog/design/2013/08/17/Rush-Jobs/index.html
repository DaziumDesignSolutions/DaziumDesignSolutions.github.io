Graphic design is sometimes a high-expectation, quick deadline industry and it can be tough. Even in a rush the smallest detail of a spelling issue will make or break a project, and you still want it to be designed well! At it's core graphic design is for the client and not the designer. It has to match the client's needs for both the individual and the collective company. For the designer, meeting these ever increasing tight deadlines means both taking responsibility and setting expectation, but it is also having the gumption, self-direction and creativity to make decisions, define the problem for the client and solve it. This problem could be time, design, writing, style, or number of concepts. That list can be large, but for the right client, the goal is to find a way to help this them through this rush. When in the middle of a rush design project it is a constant review of time compared to design possibilities. What can I design to meet client and project expectations, a design that meets my expectations of good design and still meet deadline.

As with most things, success starts with good communication. It is time management, communication and always understanding the final product. You have to consider the client is probably a bit worried about getting the project finished. As the designer, you have to be able to communicate what is reasonable to expect and what concessions have to be made to make the deadline. This skill can feel like a constant learning curve. But, when everything is in balance, rush jobs can be fun. The quick back and forth approvals are instant, with no lag time between tasks, you don't have to set aside and then relearn the project details to pick up again, and it builds an enormous amount of trust. If you solve a problem like a rush job for a client they will both give you good referrals and use you for future work.
